A brief history of Algol 68

ALGOrithmic Language Algol 68 was designed by IFIP Working Group 2.1 (Algorithmic Languages and Calculi) as a general-purpose programming language. This working group has continuing responsibility for Algol 60 and Algol 68. Algol 68 is a rather lean orthogonal general-purpose language that is a beautiful means for denoting algorithms.

In the early 1960's Working Group 2.1 started discussing the development of Algol X, a successor to Algol 60. At its 1965 meeting in Princeton, WG 2.1 invited written descriptions of the language based on the previous discussions. At the meeting in St. Pierre de Chartreuse, also in 1965, three reports describing more or less complete languages, by Wirth, Seegmüller, and Van Wijngaarden, were amongst the contributions. Other significant contributions were papers by Hoare and Naur. Van Wijngaarden's paper "Orthogonal design and description of a formal language" (available from Karl Kleine's collection) featured a new technique for language design and definition. This paper formed the basis for what would develop into Algol 68. Wirth left the project to work on Algol W which influenced his later language Pascal.

Algol 68 was defined in a formal document, first published in January 1969, and later published in Acta Informatica and also printed in Sigplan Notices. A revised report was issued in 1976. Algol 68 was the first major language for which a full formal definition was made before it was implemented.

Probably because of the formal character of the defining document, which requires some study to comprehend, the misconception got hold that Algol 68 is a complex language, while in fact it is rather lean. The reason that its definition (a "two-level grammar" or "W-grammar") looks complex is that the W-grammar describes only semantically correct programs. For example, Algol 68's W-grammar cannot describe programs that use identifiers or operators that are not declared, or that define modes resulting in objects that are infinitely large or require infinite coercion, etcetera. A context-free syntax (C, C++, Pascal, ...) describes both semantically correct and - incorrect programs, and many rules are needed besides the syntax to reject the incorrect ones. This is less elegant, but the resulting defining document looks much simpler than the Algol 68 report. You can judge this for yourself by having a look at Algol 68's context-free syntax and W-grammar.

Another factor that hindered Algol 68 from spreading rapidly is that the language was designed for programmers, not for compiler writers, in a time when the field of compiler construction was not as advanced as it is today. Implementation efforts based on formal methods generally failed; Algol 68's context-dependent grammar requires some invention to parse (consider for instance x(y, z) that can be either a call or a slice depending on the mode of x, while x does not need to be declared before being applied). Pragmatically, Algol 68 can be parsed in multiple stages, following Mailloux's scheme [Lindsey 1993]. Algol 68 Genie follows this scheme: it first identifies lexical levels, then constructs symbol tables and completes the syntax tree using bottom-up parsing and mode-checking.

At the time of Algol 68's presentation compilers usually were made available on mainframes by computing centers, which explains why Algol 68 was popular in locations rather than areas, for instance Amsterdam, Berlin or Cambridge. An important clue to understanding why Algol 68 did not become a widely used language lies in the fact that industry did not pick it up and that initiatives to commericialise Algol 68 compilers were relatively unsuccessful. The market for universal programming languages evidently did not develop as hoped for during the decade in which the language was developed and implemented.


Readily available information on Algol 68

  • Greg Nunan maintains a page on Algol 68 web resources. This page provides Programming Algol 68 Made Easy, a good informal textbook on Algol 68 written by Sian Leitch. TEX source files are in the a68toc distribution.
  • Wilhelm Kloke has made available an on-line version of the formal Revised Report, Algol 68's W-grammar extracted from same report, and C.H. Lindsey's partial parametrisation proposal.
  • HOPL - History of programming languages. On-line encyclopedia of programming languages has an entry for Algol 68.
  • Karl Kleine collects and posts Algol 68 historic documents.
  • Brian Wichmann maintains The Algol Bulletin on-line archive (subscription required).
  • The Wikipedia Project has entries for Algol 68, Algol 68 Genie, Adriaan van Wijngaarden, and two-level grammars.
  • The Algol 68 directory from the Open Directory Project.
  • Dick Grune has posted an Algol 68 program collection.
  • Norman Hardy has a page explaining ideas behind Algol 68, and an elaborated example of Algol 68 applied in tensor calculations.


Algol 68 implementations

Current other implementations

  • Algol 68S compiler by Charles Lindsey.
  • Algol 68 to C translator (a68toc), a ported compiler by Sian Leitch. The front-end essentially is the Algol 68 RS portable compiler originally written by the Defence Research Agency when it was known as the RSRE (Royal Signals and Radar Establishment). Only that part of Algol 68 needed for the ELLA compiler was implemented by a68toc; therefore formats are unavailable. Algol 68 RS did not implement the parallel-clause. The a68toc distribution is available from Greg Nunan's site.
  • Others from Greg Nunan's site or the Algol 68 directory from the Open Directory Project.

Former implementations

See for instance [AB51.3.1] or [Koster 1994]:

  • ALGOL68R. Currie et al., RSRE (Royal Signals and Radar Establishment, Malvern) 1970. Single-pass comiler, used extensively in military and scientific circles in the UK on ICL machines.
  • CONTROL DATA ALGOL 68. Zoethout et al., CDC Netherlands 1974. Full implementation (apparently it only lacked the format-pattern), used mainly in teaching in Germany and the Netherlands on CDC mainframes.
  • ALGOL68S. Hibbard et al., UK and Carnegie Mellon 1976. Subset, ported to small machines as the PDP-11, (apparently) mostly used in teaching.
  • ALGOL68RS. For ICL 2900, VAX/VMS and other systems.
  • FLACC Full Language Algol 68 Checkout Compiler. Mailloux et al., Edmonton, Canada 1978. An interpreter for the full language, available on IBM mainframes and used in teaching, both in North America and in Europe.
  • ALGOL68C. Bourne et al., Cambridge 1980. Subset implementation for IBM mainframes, DECsystems 10 and 20, VAX/UNIX, CYBER 205 and other systems. Compiler front-end that generated code for a ZCODE back-end.
